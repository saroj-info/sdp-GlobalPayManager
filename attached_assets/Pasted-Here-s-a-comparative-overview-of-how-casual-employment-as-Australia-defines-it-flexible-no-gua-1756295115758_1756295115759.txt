Here’s a comparative overview of how “casual employment” (as Australia defines it – flexible, no guaranteed hours, loading instead of benefits, termination flexibility) exists or doesn’t exist in the countries you listed:
1. Australia
Casual employment is formalised in law.
Casuals have no guaranteed hours, can decline shifts, and receive a casual loading (typically 25%) instead of paid leave.
One of the few countries with a strong, legislated casual category.
2. New Zealand
Similar to Australia but called “casual employment agreements”.
No guaranteed hours; employees work on an “as needed” basis.
Paid a higher hourly rate instead of entitlements like annual leave (holiday pay is 8% added on top).
3. United Kingdom
Equivalent is “zero-hours contracts”.
No guarantee of work; employer calls when needed.
Workers are entitled to annual leave and national minimum wage but not to predictable hours.
Controversial due to job insecurity.
4. United States
No formal casual category.
Employment is generally at-will, so workers can be terminated at any time.
Part-time, temporary, gig, or on-call work functionally fill the “casual” role.
No casual loading; benefits depend on employer, not mandated by law.
5. Japan
No casual work category like in Australia.
Uses “part-time” (arubaito) or fixed-term contracts.
Workers may lack benefits but are entitled to some protections if they meet hour thresholds.
Lifetime employment traditions still influence norms, though non-regular work has grown.
6. Philippines
“Casual employment” exists legally, but it’s defined differently.
A casual employee is hired for work not usually necessary to the business, and limited to 1 year.
If the role is continuous/necessary, the worker becomes regular (permanent).
No casual loading, but daily-rate arrangements are common.
7. Hong Kong
No official “casual” classification, but daily-rated and part-time employment exists.
Employees working more than 18 hours/week over 4 weeks gain statutory rights (holidays, MPF contributions).
Truly casual/daily workers can be excluded from some entitlements.
8. Singapore
No distinct casual category.
Uses part-time (less than 35 hrs/week) or temporary contracts.
Casual/daily-rated workers have limited rights, but if employed ≥3 months, some leave and benefits accrue.
9. Vietnam
No casual system.
Short-term contracts under 1 month are allowed but heavily regulated.
Most workers must have written contracts, even for temporary work.
Casual-style flexibility is rare.
10. India
“Casual employment” exists mainly in the unorganised/informal sector.
Labour laws recognise casual/daily-wage workers, but protections are weak.
Formal companies usually use fixed-term or contract staff instead.
Benefits and leave depend on sector and whether laws like Shops & Establishments apply.
11. Ireland
Has “casual part-time workers” but reforms have restricted zero-hours contracts.
Workers must receive a “banded hours contract” reflecting their actual hours after 12 months.
Casual work still exists but less flexible than Australia/NZ.
12. Canada
No specific casual category.
Uses part-time, temporary, seasonal, or on-call employment.
Workers are entitled to minimum employment standards (vacation pay, public holidays), but no casual loading.
Provinces regulate entitlements differently.
🔑 Key Takeaways
Closest to Australia: New Zealand, UK, Philippines (though different legal framing).
No casual loading but similar flexibility: UK (zero-hours), US (gig/at-will), Canada (on-call/seasonal), Hong Kong, Singapore.
Mostly contract or part-time alternatives: Japan, Vietnam, India, Ireland.
Unique: Only Australia has a strong legislated “casual” category with mandated casual loading.
